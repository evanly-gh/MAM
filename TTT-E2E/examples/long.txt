The study of cellular automata began in the late 1940s with the work of Stanislaw Ulam and John von Neumann, who sought a mathematical framework in which self-replication could be studied rigorously. Ulam, working at Los Alamos, had been investigating the growth of crystal-like structures on a lattice, while von Neumann was pursuing the more ambitious question of whether a machine could be designed that was capable of constructing a copy of itself from raw components. Their collaboration produced the first formal cellular automaton: a two-dimensional grid of cells, each occupying one of a finite number of states, whose evolution in discrete time steps was determined entirely by a fixed local rule applied uniformly to every cell.

The power of this formulation lay in its simplicity. Despite the starkly local nature of the update rule — each cell's next state depends only on the states of a small neighborhood around it — global patterns of extraordinary complexity can emerge. Von Neumann was able to demonstrate, though only on paper and with enormous effort, that a sufficiently rich rule set permitted the construction of a universal constructor: a configuration of cells that, given a description of an arbitrary target machine, would proceed to build it within the same cellular universe. The machine could, of course, be itself, and so self-replication was achieved within a purely formal system.

For nearly two decades after von Neumann's death the subject remained the province of specialists. Then in 1970, the British mathematician John Conway introduced a two-state, two-dimensional automaton he called the Game of Life. Conway's rules were extraordinarily simple — a dead cell with exactly three live neighbors becomes live, a live cell with two or three live neighbors survives, and all other cells die or remain dead — but the behavior of the resulting system was astonishingly rich. Gliders, still lifes, oscillators, guns, and eventually entire pattern-based computers were discovered or constructed within Life, and it became a touchstone example of how simple rules can produce unbounded complexity.

The later work of Stephen Wolfram, beginning in the 1980s, attempted to systematize the study of such systems. Wolfram classified one-dimensional cellular automata into four broad behavioral categories, ranging from those that rapidly settle into uniform or periodic states to those, most famously his Rule 110, that exhibit an intricate mixture of order and apparent randomness. Rule 110 was later proven, by Matthew Cook, to be Turing complete — a formal confirmation that arbitrarily small and simple rule sets can host universal computation.

This lineage of research, from Ulam and von Neumann through Conway and Wolfram, underlies a recurring theme in theoretical computer science and complexity studies: that the boundary between the trivial and the universal is often startlingly thin, and that emergent behavior is a generic rather than exceptional feature of locally-defined discrete dynamical systems. It is in this tradition that the modern study of self-organizing systems, including artificial life and certain branches of machine learning, situates itself.
